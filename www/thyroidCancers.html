What are Thyroid Cancers?

Thyroid Cancer is the uncontrollable growth of cells in the thyroid gland, a butterfly-shaped gland located just below the Adam's apple in the front of the neck. There are 3 different types of Thyroid Cancers: Papillary Carcinomas, Follicular Carcinomas, and Hurthle Cell Carcinomas. These 3 forms of cancer will be discussed in great detail below.

Thyroid Cancer inherently starts in a person's thyroid. The thyroid is essentially a large ductless gland in the neck that secretes hormones regulating growth and development through the rate of metabolism. The thyroid is essential for humans, because it controls how fast or hard all the essential organs work. It controls the rate of the heart, liver, stomach, lungs, and more. If the thyroid fails, it is only a matter of time before other major organs start to degrade and fail.

Let's start off by discussing each individual type of thyroid cancer. As shown through the graph below, papillary carcinomas are easily the most common types of thyroid cancer, accounting for 8/10 thyroid cancer cases. It is closely followed by follicular carcinomas, and finally, the Hurthle cell carcinomas.

Papillary Carcinomas

Papillary Carcinomas are the most frequent form of thyroid cancer, as illustrated by the chart above. Before discussing this form of cancer in detail, it is essential to understand the implications of this cancer in regard to specifically being located in the thyroid. The main purpose of the thyroid is to send hormones to other parts of the body, and regulate these hormones. This means that the thyroid has access to nearly every major organ in the body. This enables cancerous cells to spread quickly and broadly, which is why it is important to detect it in the early stages.

Papillary Carcinomas start growth in the thyroid area, and are physically indicated through a large lump on the throat, bigger than what the adams apple would be for a male. Other methods like blood tests, ultrasounds, and biopsies.

Symptoms of this form of Thyroid Cancer include intense and sharp pain in the neck, and swelling of the thyroid. If spread to the lymph nodes in the thyroid, which happens 50% of the time, swelling in these areas is also noticable.

Follicular Carcinomas

While Follicular Carcinomas only account for 17% of all thyroid cancer cases, they still cannot be ignored. Follicular Carcinomas are actually specific variants of Papillary Carcinomas, so they share many of the same symptoms and causes. Both spread to other organs easily, and both originate within the thyroid. There are a couple differences however.

Unlike papillary carcinomas, follicular carcinomas are minimally invasive. It is almost impossible to detect these types of carcinomas physically, and in order to really prognose the situation, things like blood tests and ultrasounds must be scheduled.

Another key difference between the two is that follicular carcinomas rarely spread to the lymph nodes. This opens up a wide variety of non-invasive surgery, that is still complicated, but much better than having to operate on teh lymph nodes.

Hurthle Cell Carcinomas

Hurthle Cell Carcinomas (HCC) are the rarest form of thyroid cancer, but cannot be ignored because of the implications it holds. Just as FC are variants of PC, HCC are variants of FC, going a step further. This form of cancer attacks Hurthle Cells, cells ripe with cytoplasm, which are infected and diable the mitochondria. This in turn reduces the amount of energy being given to the body, delibitating the health of a patient, and slowly killing them.

While many of the symptoms do not differ from the other two forms of cancer discussed above, the most important thing to take into consideration is how easily it can spread. The true symptoms of this tragic disease lie in how easily it spreads in the body, affecting the organs necessary for survival in every human.

Since 1973, the incidence rates of Thyroid Cancer have been skyrocketing. While on the surface this may look bad, modern science and vast innovations in medicine and technology have kept the death rate extremely steady, even though the incidence rate keeps rising. This shows that because of these new innovations discussed below, the world was able to curb the violent effects of Thyroid Cancer, and will continue to do so through everyone's support.

Medical Staff

Once a patient is diagnosed with any form of Thyroid Cancer, the best doctors and other medical staff work with the patient to create the best possible treatment plan. There are a total of 3 specialized doctors that work with thyroid cancers. The oncologist is a general cancer doctor that establishes the optimal treatment plan for the patient. The nuclear medicine doctor is a doctor who specializes in applying radioactive substances to diagnose and treat various thyroid carcinomas. Lastly, the Otolaryngologist is a doctor who specializes in treatment of the ear, nose, and throat area.

Depending on the type and stage of the cancer and other factors, treatment options for lung cancer can include: Surgery, radiation therapy, laser therapy, chemotherapy, and certain types of targeted therapy. Sometimes, more than one of type of treatment might be used. In choosing which treatment plan is best for the patient, some important factors to consider include the location and stage of the cancer, the patient's overall health, the chances of curing the disease, and the possible impact of the treatment on the thyroid.

Radioactive Iodine

This new development in the thyroid cancer treatment industry refferred to as Radioactive Iodine, is unique in its own way, because thyroid cells are the only cell type in the body that possess the mechanisms to absorb and concentrate iodine. Physicians can take advantage of this fact and give radioactive iodine to patients as a treatment option for papillary thyroid cancer.

There are several types of radioactive iodine, with one type being toxic to cells. Papillary thyroid cancer cells absorb iodine; therefore, they can be destroyed by giving the toxic isotope (I-131).

This type of treatment is such a huge breakthrough in modern science, especially when considered that it is considered a form of chemotherapy, without all the negatives like naseua, hair loss, and others.

Thyroidectomy

Thyroidectomies usually involve removal of all or most of the thyroid. This is an especially new form of treatment, as scientists and doctors were not able to emulate the herculean job of the thyroid, but are able to do so now and remove the infected area before any of the cancerous cells spread.nimally invasive treatment for cancer. It is an image-guided technique that heats and destroys cancer cells.

Regardless of whether a patient has just one thyroid lobe and the isthmus removed, or the entire thyroid gland removed, most experts agree they should be placed on thyroid hormone replacement for the rest of their lives. This replaces the hormone in those who have no thyroid left, and to suppress further growth of the gland in those with some tissue left in the neck.

This is only scratching the surface of what modern science has brought the world in terms of treatment options, and we hope that you are able to further this process as well, until we finally crack the case of 100% cancer treatment.
