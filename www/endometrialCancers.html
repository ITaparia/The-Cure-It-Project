What are Endometrial Cancers?

Endometrial Cancer is described as the uncontrollable growth of cells, originating in the lining of the womb (uterus). There are 2 different types of Endometrial Cancers: Endometrial Carcinomas, and Uterine Sarcomas. These 2 forms of cancer will be discussed in great detail below.

Endometrial Cancers start in the uterus, or the cells that outline the uterus. Because of this, it only affects females. Endometrial Cancer can affect a woman's fertility, in addtion to the danger of metastasizing, or spreading.

Let's start off by discussing each individual type of Endometrial Cancer. As shown through the graph below, Endometrial Carcinomas are easily the most common types of endometrial cancer, accounting for 92% of all bone cancer cases. Uterine Sarcomas make up the rest of the cases.

Endometrial Carcinomas

Endometrial Carcinomas start in the inner lining of the uterus. There are two layers of the uterus. The inner layer is called the endometrium, and the outer layer is called the myometrium. Endometrial Carcinomas would be the uncontrollabel cell growth in the endometrium.

Many scientists have come to the conclusion that the primary cause of this form of Endometrial Cancer is due to increased estrogen in the body. 95% of this form of Endometrial Cancer turns out to be Type 1, which means that the outlook is generally positive, and the tumor itself isn't very aggressive. The main harm comes about when the tumor spreads throguhout the body, and Type 1 Endometrial Cancers typically do metastasize(spread) easily.

An easy to spot indicator of Endometrial Cancer in general is indicated by abnormal vaginal bleeding. This may mean a drastic change in period time, or bleeding between periods. Of course, there are a number of other problems that could cause this, but when spotted, it is important to contact a doctor immediately. Vaginal discharge not including blood can also be a clear sign of this form of cancer.

Uterine Sarcomas

Uterine Sarcomas are much less common than Endometrial Carcinomas and are very similar to them in all ways except a few. Uterine Sarcomas do start in the endometrium, but they are also much more aggressive than Endometrial Carcinomas. They are considered to be the Type 2 form of Endometrial Carcinomas. It is much easier for Uterine Carcinomas to spread so doctors treat this form much more aggressively.

There are many symptoms of this form of cancer including vaginal bleeding, pain in the cervix especially during intercourse, massive weight loss, and widespread fatiuge. There is also the danger of the cancer metastasizing, which is more of a threat with Uterine Sarcomas than Endometrial Carcinomas.



Advancements in Treatment

Since 1975, the death rates of Endometrial Cancer have taken a serious hit. While the incidence rate has stayed mostly the same, new advances in science and technology are ultimately saving more lives. Check out a graph of this progression below..

Why the Increase?

Due to the extreme advances in modern science and practice of medicine, the death rate for Endometrial Cancers have taken a significant hit. All the improvements we have collectively made have laid paths for new forms of treatment, which will be described below, in addition to the specialists that typically take care of Endometrial Cancer patients.

Medical Staff

Once a patient is diagnosed with any form of Endometrial Cancer, the best doctors and other medical staff work with the patient to create the best possible treatment plan. The oncologist is a general cancer doctor that establishes the optimal treatment plan for the patient. The nuclear medicine doctor is a doctor who specializes in applying radioactive substances to diagnose and treat various endometrial tumors. Lastly, the OBGYN Doctor is a doctor who focuses on reproductive health in women and childbirth, and studies the behavior of the uterus in terms of how it reacts to the cancer.

Depending on the type and stage of the cancer and other factors, treatment options for Endometrial Cancer can include: Surgery(hysterectomy), radiation therapy, hormonal therapy, and chemotherapy. Sometimes, more than one of type of treatment might be used. In choosing which treatment plan is best for the patient, some important factors to consider include the location and stage of the cancer, the patient's overall health, the chances of curing the disease, and the possible impact of the treatment on the uterus.

Hysterectomy

For many women, surgery is basically the go-to treatment for Endometrial Cancer of any form. The specific type of surgery that is done is called a hysterectomy. A hysterectomy involves removing the uterus and cervix. The fallopian tubes and ovaries are also removed, but it is part of another operation, usually done in tandem with a hysterectomy.

The main point behind a hysterectomy is to treat Type 1 Endometrial Carcinomas. Because Type 1 tumors do not spread easily, the entire uterus is removed to completely eradicate all cancerous cells from the body.

Over the years, there has been much work done to improve this form of surgery to lead to higher success rates. Medical facilities all around the world have started to use robotic systems to guide these surgerys, with precision and accuracy, leading to higher success rates than any human in the world.

Hormone Therapy

A new development in the area of Endometrial Cancer treatment is the usage of hormones. More specifically, the progestin family of hormones. These hormones cause the cancerous cells to react in certain ways, in order to stop the spread of endometrial cancer.

Medroxyprogesterone acetate (clinically known as Provera) and megestrol acetate (clinically known as Megace) are two drugs that have been recently engineered through advanced medical research and technology. The purpose of these two drugs is to rapidly slow and actually destroy the cancerous cells in the uterus. These drugs are often combined to reach powerful effects that rarely affect the patient herself.
