What are Pancreatic Cancers?

Pancreatic Cancers are among the deadliest forms of cancers out there. While it is relatively rare compared to other forms of cancer, the prognosis is very dire. Pancreatic cancer often has a poor prognosis, even when diagnosed early. Pancreatic cancer typically spreads rapidly and is seldom detected in its early stages, which is a major reason why it's a leading cause of cancer death. There are two major types of pancreatic cancer: Exocrine Tumors, and Endocrine Tumors. Both types will be discussed throughly below.

Pancreatic Cancer comprise of tumors found in or near the pancreatic region of your body. The pancreas is a 6-inch long organ located behind the stomach in the back of the abdomen. It's function in the body is essential: it secretes hormones and sends them to glands in order to perform routine body functions like digesting fat and protein, spreading oxygen to the body, and producing excitatory hormones like dopamine and adrenaline. Pancreatic Cancer involves the uncontrollable growth of pancreatic cells that form tumors that impede normal functions of the pancreas to the point where a person is no longer able to function normally.

Exocrine Cancers

Exocrine Tumors are easily the most common form of pancreatic cancer known. If anyone is diagnosed with pancreatic cancer, it is almost always referring to this specific type of tumor. Differentiating between Exocrine and Endocrine Tumors are very important, because each one is different in their own ways. Each one has a different prognosis, method of treatment, and outlook. Before delving into the specifics of exocrine tumors, first understand the workings of the pancreas.

The exocrine glands make pancreatic “juice,” which is released into the intestines. This juice contains enzymes that help you digest the food you eat. Without these, some of the food would just pass through your intestines without being absorbed. The enzymes are released into tiny tubes called ducts. These tiny ducts merge to form larger ducts, which empty into the pancreatic duct. The tumors block these passageways, restricting and sometimes even completely cutting off the flow of these tubes, which renders the body useless because of the lack of enzymes and hormones.

The thing that makes Exocrine Cancers so deadly is the fact that it is connected to literally every single major functioning system in the body. Remember when we said that the enzymes and hormones produced by the pancreas travel to other organs to make them function? This gives the malignant tumors access to every single system in your body, which is why it metastasizes (spreads) so quickly.

Another reason why its so deadly is the fact that it is hard to detect early on. Even when pancreatic is pre-emptively found, its usually in the later stages and usually is so bad, that little can be done to reduce the mortality chance.

Endocrine Cancer

Endocine Tumors occur significantly less than exocrine tumors because only about 5% of cells in the pancreas are Enteroendocrine (Endocrine) Cells. It is important to differentiate between Exocrine and Endocrine Tumors. For one, Exocrine Tumors have the ability to spread to other systems in the body, while endocrine tumors are able to spread only within the pancreas itself, limiting the mortality rate significantly.

Another key difference between the two are their symptoms. Exocrine Cancers, like stated obove, affect the entire body, which can lead to anything ranging from yellow skin and eyes, fluid in the abdomen, extreme weight loss, to dark urine. Endocrine Cancers on the other hand, can lead to symoptoms more related to moods and feelings, because of the concentration inside the pancreas, which produces hormones to do with feelings. Things like irritability, severe depression, anxiety, and changes in bodily habits are all part of the numerous detriments of endocrine tumors.

Why the Increase?

The decrease shown above can only be attributed to the massive strides humanity as a whole is taking in improving science and technology, to make a quicker diagnosis, and improvements in treatment methods, to help get rid of the cancer as quickly as possible once diagnosed. Take a look below to see some of the types of doctors involved in pancreatic cancer treatment, and other methods of treatment proven to work.

Medical Staff

Once a patient is diagnosed with any form of Pancreatic Cancer, the best doctors and other medical staff work with the patient to create the best possible treatment plan. There are a total of 4 specialized doctors that work with pancreatic cancers. The radiation oncologist is a general cancer doctor that establishes the optimal treatment plan by using radiation treatment techniques. An endocrinologist is a doctor who treats diseases in glands that secrete hormones. This doctor mostly helps with the diagnosis and prognosis of the patient in question. There will also be a surgeon on the patients team, which will assist in removing the tumor manually if there is no other choice. Finally, the medical oncologist will specialize in chemotherapy and other medicines in order to assist the removal of the cancer.

Depending on the type and stage of the cancer and other factors, treatment options for pancreatic cancer can include: Palliative Surgery, radiation therapy, chemotherapy, or other clinical trials of new cancer-reducing medicines. Sometimes, more than one of type of treatment might be used. In choosing which treatment plan is best for the patient, some important factors to consider include the location and stage of the cancer, the patient's overall health, the chances of curing the disease, and the possible impact of the treatment on the pancreas.

Palliative Surgery

Palliative Surgery Is less of a treatment method, and used mostly as a pain reliever, so that other treatment methods can be used. It involves the remove an intestinal obstruction or remove masses that are causing pain or disfigurement

Palliative surgery on the pancreas usually involves unblocking the pancreatic ducts, so that while the tumor isnt completely gone, other methods like radiation and chemotherapy can be used.

This type of surgery usually doesn't have any side effects, because it is only meant to relieve pain, but the methods of treatment used AFTER this surgery could have sever side effects.

External Beam Radiation Therapy

External Beam Radiation Therapy is a method of radiation therapy used to get rid of most tumors, including pancreatic cancers.

Beams are generated outside the patient (usually by a linear accelerator, see below) and are targeted at the tumor site. These high energy x-rays can deposit their dose to the area of the tumor to destroy the cancer cells and, with careful treatment planning, spare the surrounding normal tissues. No radioactive sources are placed inside the patient's body.

This type of therapy is mostly used when the cancer has spread from the pancreas to other parts of the body, since it is most effective with a wide range of targets.
