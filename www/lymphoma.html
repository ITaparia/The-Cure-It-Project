What is Lymphoma?

Lymphoma is essentially an uncontrollable growth of a specific type of white blood cell called lymphatic cells. There are 2 main types of Lymphoma: Hodgkin's Disease, and Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma. These 2 forms of Lymphoma will be discussed in great detail below.

Since Lymphoma affects the lymphatic system, it directly harms the immune system. Lymphatic cells are a type of white blood cell, which attack pathogens, or bacteria and viruses, the moment they enter the body. Once these white blood cells are rendered useless through Lymphoma, the body is not able to fend off small viruses like the common cold, and patients become sick very often.

Let's start off by discussing each individual type of Lymphoma. As shown through the graph below, Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma(NHL) is much more common than Hodgkin's Disease. NHL is the 6th most common cancer in men, and 5th most common in women as of 2014, in contrast to Hodgkin's Disease, which only accounts for 1% of all cancers in the US.

Non - Hodgkin's Lymphoma

NHL is the more common form of lymphoma, and is actually the 6th most common form of cancer in men, and 5th in women, posing a serious problem to everyone. NHL is essentially the uncontrollable growth of a specific type of cell called a lymphocyte.

Lympphocytes can be broken up into two types of cells, B-Cells and T-Cells. B-Cells make antibodies, and fight against common germs like bacteria and viruses. These B-Cells use the antibodies they create to latch onto germs and mark them to be destroyed by T-Cells. T-Cells perform the actual destruction of harmful germs by digesting germs or boosting the performance of other T-Cells by secreting chemicals. NHL renders these types of cells useless, opening the body up to more attacks, and becoming weaker in defending them at the same time.

The real danger of NHL comes from the fact that it can spread so easily to places with lymphocytes, like the spleen, digestive tract, bone marrow, and many others. Symptoms may include swollen lymph nodes, severe pain in the joints, chest, or abdomen, whole body fatigue, and many more.

Hodgkin's Disease

Hodgkin's Disease(HD) attacks the same areas that NHL does: Lymphocytes. HD takes over these cells found in the immune system, and renders them weakend or useless, making the body more prone to sicknesses. Because of this, the symptoms for HD are very similar to NHL. There is one key difference however, which can only be seen under a microscope.

The real difference between NHL and HD is the type of lymphocyte affected. Under close scrutiny of a doctor's microscope, one can tell the difference between NHL and HD if the lymphocyte affected is a Reed - Sternberg Cell. RS cells are very large compared to other types of cells, and are typically considered to be a sub set of B-Cells, performing functions much slower and less efficiently. If there are multitudes of thise type of cell in the lymphatic system, it is a clear sign of HD.

Why the Increase?

Due to the extreme advances in modern science and practice of medicine, the death rate for both NHL and HD have taken a significant hit. All the improvements we have collectively made have laid paths for new forms of treatment, which will be described below, in addition to the specialists that typically take care of Lymphoma patients.

Medical Staff

Once a patient is diagnosed with any form of Endometrial Cancer, the best doctors and other medical staff work with the patient to create the best possible treatment plan. The oncologist is a general cancer doctor that establishes the optimal treatment plan for the patient. The medical oncologist specializes in treating cancer with chemotherapy and various chemicals that have been proven to aid with lymphoma. Lastly, the Hematology pathologist is a doctor who focuses on the diagnosing and treatment of blood diseases, in this case, types of Lymphoma.

Depending on the type and stage of the cancer and other factors, treatment options for Lymphoma can include: Chemotherapy, Immunotherapy, and certain forms of Targeted Therapy. Sometimes, more than one of type of treatment might be used. In choosing which treatment plan is best for the patient, some important factors to consider include the location and stage of the cancer, the patient's overall health, the chances of curing the disease, and the possible impact of the treatment on the blood and lymphatic system.

Immunotherapy

Immunotherapy is a form of therapy recently created, that is specific to treating the lymphatic system, and the system that gets hurt with Lymphoma: the immune system.

This works through a process of using Monoclonal Antibodies, which are designed to target cancer cells and recruit immune cells to attack. The entire point behind this is to strengthen the immune system, to fight against cancerous lymphocytes.

Recent developments in science have allowed us to discover medicines like mogamulizumab and milatuzumab, which are able to not only strengthen a patients immune system, but target only cancerous cells, leaving the body stronger than ever before and free of cancer.

Chemotherapy

Over the years, chemotherapy has been refined through many endeavors of scientific communities all around the world, and for Lymphoma, many new chemicals have been created to target cancerous cells specifically, while leaving a patient healthier in the long run.

Chemicals like Cyclophosphamide (clinically known as Cytoxan) and Bendamustine (clinically known as Treanda) are all newly developed and are treating more lymphoma patients than ever before.
