What are Bone Cancers?

Bone Cancer is described as the uncontrollable growth of cells, originating in areas with bones or bone related cells. There are 3 different types of Bone Cancers: Osteosarcomas, Ewing Tumors(ET), and Chondrosarcomas. These 3 forms of cancer will be discussed in great detail below.

Bone Cancers, as the name implies, are abnormal clusters of cells found inside bones, and lead to the death of normal bone tissue. The real danger in this type of cancer is how easily it can spread, or metastasize around the body. Since bones exist in practically every system and area of the body, this disease has the potential to be spread almost anywhere.

Let's start off by discussing each individual type of bone cancer. As shown through the graph below, Osteosarcomas are easily the most common types of bone cancer, accounting for 56% of all bone cancer cases. This is followed by Ewing Tumors, and finally, Chondrosarcomas.

Osteosarcomas

Osteosarcomas are the most frequent form of bone cancer, as illustrated by the chart above. Before discussing this form of cancer in detail, it is essential to understand the implications of this cancer in regard to specifically being located in the skeletal system. It is pretty apparent to most people what the skeletal system does for the body: protection and support. The only reason why the body is able to move the way it is is due to the joints and bones located all over the body.

The fact that bones are located everywhere on the body is what makes this form of cancer so dangerous. Osteosarcomas can technically occur anyplace where a bone exists, but most commonly forms near the lower thigh (femur), shin bone (tibia), and the upper arm (humerus). The exact causes of bone cancer are unknown, but it is known that genetics play a key factor in the transmission of this disease.

Symptoms of bone cancer vary, depending on the location and size of the tumor. The most common ones include intense pain at tumor site, rare bone fractures, breathing issues, and unexplained weight loss.

Ewing Tumors

Ewing Tumors account for over 30% of all bone cancer cases. They usually form in the same spots as osteosarcomas, with two exceptions. They can form in the cavities of the leg and arm bones, and can also form in soft tissues of the body near bones.

Ewing Tumors are a bit different than Osteosarcomas, because they form from primitive cells, the oldest cells in the body. This doesnt mean they are treated differently: Ewing tumors still contain the same DNA abnormailities, and respond to all methods of treatment the same way other cancerous cells do.

As of now, there are no environmental factors that contribute to the development of Ewing Tumors. The only way to protect someone against this disease is to make sure that the person is diagnosed as soon as possible

Chondrosarcomas

Chondrosarcomas are different than the other forms of bone cancer in one very specific way. Instead of infecting hard bones, Chondrosarcomas actually go for the cartilage in the body. They can still form in the hips, ribs and arm bones, but the key difference is that they can attack places with cartilage.

Because of the fact that this type of Bone Cancer can affect cartilage, symptoms are the same as osteosarcomas, but with the addition of olfactory (smell) and auditory (hearing) loss, because those two areas are made up of mostly cartilage.



Advancements in Treatment

Since 1975, the incidence rates of Bone Cancer have been increasing, not by a massive amount, but still significant enough to be affecting more people. At first glance, this seems like a bad situation. The truth is, in fact, the opposite. Through data, it can be seen that even though the incidence rate has slightly increased, modern medicine and science has forced the death rate to absolutely plunge in these recent decades, through new improved means of treatment and diagnosis that are helping people all across the planet.

Depending on the type and stage of the cancer and other factors, treatment options for bone cancer can include: Surgery, radiation therapy (proton therapy), laser therapy, chemotherapy, and certain types of targeted therapy. Sometimes, more than one of type of treatment might be used. In choosing which treatment plan is best for the patient, some important factors to consider include the location and stage of the cancer, the patient's overall health, the chances of curing the disease, and the possible impact of the treatment on the bones.

Proton Therapy

This new development in the bone cancer treatment industry refferred to as Proton Therapy offers an alternative to complete surgery, where the bone in question simply cannot be removed or shifted.

In terms of bone cancer, proton therapy is especially revolutionary because it concentrates a beam of focused protons at the diseased section of the bone. It provides enough radiation to kill the cancerous cells, while sparing the surrounding cells.

This type of treatment is such a huge breakthrough in modern science, especially when considered that it is considered a form of radiation, without all the negatives consequences.

Myeloablative Therapy

Myeloablative Therapy Is one of the most interesting treatment types that have been invented in the past couple of years. This type of treatment uses strong forms of chemotherapy to attack cancerous cells, especially when spread throughout the body.

This form of cancer is unique because it utilizes a new form of treatment that has been becoming more popular over the past couple of years: stem cells. Traditionally, concentrated chemotherapy would cause the marrow in the bones to be destroyed. Unfortunately during this treatment they are. However, this specific form of therpay actually enables stems cells to come in, replace the bone marrow that was damaged or destroyed, giving a foundation for the patient, while still keeping the other healthy cells alive.

Bone Cancer Research Trust

The Bone Cancer Research Trust is a foundation dedicated to stop the spread of Bone Cancer, and prevent it from limiting people from achieving their dreams. They began in 2006, and aim to provide support for research of bone cancers, raising awareness, providing information, and giving support to the patients already inflicted with bone cancer.

The BCRT has funded 35 peer-reviewed research projects since 2006, totalling £1,392,731, which equates to roughly $2,114,792. This massive sum of money has all gone towards the betterment of lives of people with bone cancer, and providing research in orderto prevent bone cancer from spreading even more.
